History

Captain Barclay was one of the first modern enthusiasts of ultra-long performances, when in 1806 he went 160 km in 19 hours. Three years later, one mile (1609 m) ran every hour, and it was a continuous one thousand hours in a row. Barclay was a smart and unusual man. In his 21 years he trained at farmer Jackey Smith, who trained many of the excellent English pedestrians on long tracks. Smith's training program consisted of four to six intensive weeks prior to important walking, walking, and heavy mental stress.

Smith was a tough coach and once prepared Barclay for a 110 mile race (176 km). His training bouts ranged from 32 to 38 km every day. He had to get up at five o'clock in the morning, run half a mile at his highest speed, and then go six miles to race speed. After breakfast he had to go six miles. After lunch he repeated his morning training (half a mile at the maximum running speed and six miles of racing). After three to four weeks, trained athletes ran at a maximum pace four miles instead of the original half a mile.

Marathon Des Sables

Marathon des Sables is considered to be one of the world's toughest ultras, the temperature can rise up to 50 ° C during the day to fall to 5 ° C at night. The race is attended by 1,200 competitors from 48 countries each year, and for many competitors it is the turn of the season.
